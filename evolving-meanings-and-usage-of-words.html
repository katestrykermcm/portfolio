Evolving Meanings & Usage of Words

CHALLENGE   |   PROCESS   |   RESULTS

How do words evolve over time? How are these changes reflected in the daily articles of The New York Times, published continuously since 1851?

Tools: JavaScript (d3.js, jQuery), NYTimes API, HTML/CSS

Skills: Data Retrieval, Visualization Ideation, Web Design & Development, Paper Prototyping

Duration: 1 month in 2017

Team: Madison Borgmann

Link: Final Project

Inspiration

We were initially intrigued by this topic after coming across a variety of graphs that plotted the percent of New York Times articles in which a particular word appeared over time. The majority of these were created using Alexis Lloyd's Chronicle Tool. In the red and yellow bar graph (bottom left), Jer Thorp also visualized the terms “terrorism” (above the axis) and “communism” (below the axis) from 1981 to 2009. “Terrorism” spikes in 2001, following September 11th, and “communism” gradually decreases. The yellow bars indicate the occurences that were on the front page. Despite their simplicity, these graphs were able to powerfully convey what the paper (and country) cared about.

Identifying Words to Explore

To identify more words that could be of interest to plot and analyze, we referred to First Words, The New York Time Magazines’s weekly essay series about “what language reveals about our moment.” We read every fascinating article (honestly!) and developed a list of many words and phrases to explore.

Retrieving the Data

As Alexis Lloyd's Chronicle Tool is no longer available to the public, we made our own! The New York Time's Article Search API empowered us to retrieve New York Times articles from 1851 to today, including headlines, abstracts, lead paragraphs, links to associated multimedia, and more. The API only allows users to process 1 item asynchronously every second and we made a request for each year for every word on our list, so it took a while to acquire the data. However, once we saved it to our machine, it was quick and easy to access.

Plotting All Words of Interest

We created graphs of all of the words on our list. We used the same axes as the graphs that initially inspired us: the percent of total articles containing the word vs. time, from 1851 to today.

Narrowing in

At this point, our scope was too large. While the many words we plotted were arguably compelling individually, we were struggling to collectively present anything meaningful. Therefore, we decided to focus on just 2 words that we found particularly striking because of their complex meanings, recent widespread use, and persistent, interconnected role in history: “radical” and “progressive.”

Interpreting Trends

These graphs tell a story, but what is it? We referred back to the First Words essays and incorporated historical research of our own in order to understand the cultural context that shaped the trends. Ultimately, we generated short explanations for each notable peak and valley in both graphs.

Incorporating Sentiment Analysis

Beyond observing trends in the occurrence of the 2 words in New York Times articles, we wanted to determine the tone that accompanied their usage. Were journalists depicting the words positively or negatively? We turned to sentiment analysis of the articles' lead paragraphs to help us begin to answer these kinds of questions. It was disappointing that we could not run the analysis on the entire article, but the API's query options limited us to this. We sought the help of a variety of sentiment analysis tools, including LIWC2015, Lexalytics, and LightSide.

Overview

Words are adaptable. Their connotations can differ greatly between people, times, places, etc. They change as the culture in which they are used changes. A “radical” in 1865 isn’t necessarily what a modern American would define as radical. Does a modern conservative know that some of their economic policies originated with the Progressive Party in the 1900’s? Conflict and disagreement aren’t new to American society, but neither are many of the words at the center of these arguments. This project reveals, through data driven visualizations of article data from The New York Times, how two highly used words in American society, “radical” and “progressive,” have evolved over time. The martini-glass format guides users through timeline graphs of the words by examining historical events surrounding each major peak and valley. Visit the project site!

Next Steps

To provide even further context for the stories of the evolving meanings of "progressive" and "radical," we will take advantage of The New York Times API's article links, headline titles, lead paragraphs, and associated multimedia. We've started to do this, but it's still a work in progress.
